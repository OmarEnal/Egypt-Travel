About Faiyom

Fayoum is a unique and historically significant region located about 100 kilometers southwest of Cairo. It is one of the oldest continuously inhabited areas in Egypt and is known for its natural beauty, ancient heritage, and agricultural richness. Fayoum is technically an oasis, although it is connected to the Nile River through a network of canals, the most famous being Bahr Youssef.

In ancient times, Fayoum was known as "Crocodilopolis", named for the worship of the crocodile god Sobek. The city was once a thriving center of religion and agriculture during the Pharaonic and Greco-Roman periods. The region still holds many fascinating archaeological sites, including Hawara Pyramid, the ruins of Karanis, and the Temple of Sobek at Kiman Faris.

One of the most famous natural landmarks in Fayoum is Lake Qarun, Egypt’s third-largest lake and a key destination for birdwatching, fishing, and relaxing by the water. Nearby lies the Wadi El-Rayan nature reserve, known for its beautiful waterfalls, desert landscapes, and rare wildlife. This area also includes Wadi Al-Hitan ("Valley of the Whales"), a UNESCO World Heritage Site famous for its well-preserved fossils of prehistoric whales, offering a glimpse into the region’s ancient marine past.

Fayoum is also famous for its traditional pottery, handmade crafts, and authentic rural villages. The town of Tunis Village has become a hub for artists and potters, attracting visitors interested in culture, nature, and eco-tourism. The area’s green fields, palm groves, and water wheels (saqiyas) reflect its deep agricultural heritage, earning it the nickname “the breadbasket of Egypt.”

Today, Fayoum is a peaceful destination that offers a balance between history, nature, and local culture. It is ideal for those who want to explore a quieter side of Egypt, away from the hustle of big cities, while still being surrounded by stunning landscapes and ancient stories.
