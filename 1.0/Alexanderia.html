About Alexandria

Alexandria is a historic and vibrant city located on the northern coast of Egypt, along the Mediterranean Sea. It is the second-largest city in Egypt, after Cairo, and serves as a major port, cultural center, and economic hub for the country. Founded in 331 BC by Alexander the Great, the city quickly rose to prominence as a center of knowledge, science, and trade in the ancient world.

In ancient times, Alexandria was home to some of the most famous landmarks in history, including the Great Library of Alexandria, which was one of the largest and most significant libraries of the ancient world, and the Lighthouse of Alexandria (Pharos), one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. These landmarks made the city a symbol of learning, innovation, and civilization.

Throughout its long history, Alexandria has been influenced by various cultures, including Greek, Roman, Byzantine, Islamic, and modern Egyptian. This rich blend of influences is reflected in its architecture, food, and diverse population. Some of the key historical and cultural sites in the city today include the Catacombs of Kom El Shoqafa, the Roman Amphitheatre, the Citadel of Qaitbay, and the modern Bibliotheca Alexandrina, a tribute to the ancient library.

Modern Alexandria is known for its beautiful beaches, coastal promenade (Corniche), and vibrant city life. It offers a unique mix of the old and the new, where ancient ruins stand alongside modern shopping centers, cafés, and universities. The city also plays an important role in Egypt’s education system, with several prominent universities and research institutions.

Alexandria remains a favorite destination for both Egyptian and international tourists, thanks to its moderate Mediterranean climate, rich history, and cultural charm. It is often called the “Pearl of the Mediterranean” and continues to be a place where history and modernity coexist in harmony.
