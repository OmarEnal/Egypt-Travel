About Luxor

Luxor is one of the most remarkable and historically rich cities in Egypt, often referred to as the “world’s greatest open-air museum.” Located in Upper Egypt along the banks of the Nile River, Luxor stands on the site of the ancient city of Thebes, which was the capital of Egypt during the height of its power in the New Kingdom (c. 1550–1070 BC).

Luxor is divided by the Nile into two parts: the East Bank, which represents the world of the living, and the West Bank, symbolizing the land of the dead. Each side is filled with awe-inspiring monuments that showcase the grandeur and spirituality of ancient Egyptian civilization.

On the East Bank, visitors will find the Karnak Temple Complex, one of the largest religious structures ever built. It is dedicated primarily to the god Amun-Ra and features massive columns, obelisks, statues, and sacred lakes. Nearby is the elegant Luxor Temple, connected to Karnak by the recently restored Avenue of Sphinxes, which once hosted grand religious processions.

The West Bank is home to some of the most famous archaeological treasures in the world. These include the Valley of the Kings, where over 60 royal tombs have been discovered, including the tomb of Tutankhamun, filled with stunning artifacts. Also on the West Bank are the Valley of the Queens, the Temple of Hatshepsut (a unique mortuary temple built into the cliffs), and the Colossi of Memnon, two massive seated statues that guard the Theban necropolis.

Luxor is not only about ancient history; it is also a vibrant modern city with a strong connection to Egyptian culture and tradition. Visitors can enjoy felucca rides on the Nile, explore bustling souks, or attend sound and light shows at the temples. The city plays a vital role in Egypt’s tourism industry and hosts frequent archaeological discoveries and international missions.
