About Sinai

Sinai is a unique and historically significant region located in the northeastern part of Egypt. It is a triangular-shaped peninsula that serves as a land bridge between Africa and Asia, bordered by the Mediterranean Sea to the north and the Red Sea to the south. Known for its dramatic landscapes, rich religious history, and strategic importance, Sinai is a region of both natural beauty and spiritual depth.

One of the most iconic landmarks in Sinai is Mount Sinai (Jebel Musa), traditionally believed to be the place where Moses received the Ten Commandments. It is a sacred site for Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, and attracts pilgrims and tourists from around the world. At the foot of the mountain lies Saint Catherineâ€™s Monastery, one of the oldest working Christian monasteries in the world, built in the 6th century and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The region is divided into two governorates: North Sinai and South Sinai. While the northern part is more arid and sparsely populated, South Sinai is known for its popular resort towns such as Sharm El-Sheikh, Dahab, Nuweiba, and Taba. These coastal cities on the Red Sea are famous for their crystal-clear waters, coral reefs, and diving spots, making them some of the top destinations for snorkeling, scuba diving, and eco-tourism in the world.

Sinai also boasts stunning natural landscapes, including rugged mountains, colorful canyons, desert valleys, and oases. Notable sites include the Coloured Canyon, the Blue Hole in Dahab, and Ras Mohammed National Park, a protected area with rich marine biodiversity.

Culturally, Sinai is home to the Bedouin people, who have lived in the desert for centuries. Their traditions, crafts, hospitality, and deep connection to the land add a unique cultural layer to the region. Visitors often enjoy Bedouin-style dinners, camel rides, and guided desert tours.
